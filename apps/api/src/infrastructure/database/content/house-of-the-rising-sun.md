## About this piece
"The House of the Rising Sun" is a traditional folk song with **no single known composer** — it grew and changed over generations through oral tradition (catalogued as Roud 6393). Its roots probably lie in old English broadside ballads carried to America by emigrants, where it was reset in New Orleans. The earliest surviving recording dates to 1934 (Clarence Ashley and Gwen Foster), and the song became world-famous through The Animals' 1964 arrangement. Because it belongs to everyone, countless versions exist — treat it as a shape to make your own.

## What to listen for
A brooding tale of a life gone wrong, set to a rolling 6/8 lilt. The famous minor-key arpeggio pattern gives it its haunting, circling character, each chord flowing into the next like a slow waltz-in-two.

## What you'll learn
Arpeggiated chord accompaniment in 6/8, smooth chord changes, and steady fingerpicking. It's an ideal early fingerstyle song because the pattern repeats over changing chords, letting you focus on one hand at a time.

## Chords, keys and approach
Commonly played in **A minor**. A widely used progression is **Am – C – D – F – Am – C – E** (some versions end the cycle on E7). Capo and key vary with the singer. **Fingerpicking** suits it best: roll a broken-chord pattern (bass note then higher strings) through each chord in the 6/8 feel. It also works with a gentle arpeggiated strum. Keep the tempo unhurried and let the bass notes ring.

## How to practise
Learn the chord shapes and changes first, strumming once per chord in time. Then add the arpeggio, keeping the thumb steady on the bass. Practise the trickiest change (often to F) in isolation. Sing or hum the melody so your accompaniment breathes with the words.
