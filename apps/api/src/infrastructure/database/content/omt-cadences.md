## Punctuation for music

A **cadence** is a short harmonic formula that closes a phrase. If a melody is a sentence, cadences are its punctuation: some land like a full stop, others hang open like a comma, and one sets you up for a surprise. Learning to hear them is the fastest way to feel *where* a piece of music breathes.

Every cadence is defined by the **last two chords** of the phrase and by how strongly they resolve. Four of them cover almost everything you will meet at first.

## Authentic cadence (V–I)

The **authentic cadence** moves from the dominant to the tonic — `V–I` (or `V7–I`). It is the strongest close in tonal music because the dominant is packed with tension that only the tonic relieves. In C major that is `G–C`.

Two things make it decisive:

- The **leading tone** (scale degree 7, `B` in C major) sits in the `V` chord and pulls up a half-step to the tonic `C`.
- If there is a seventh (`G7`), its `F` falls to `E`, so the tritone `B–F` resolves outward-and-inward at once.

A **perfect** authentic cadence (PAC) puts both chords in root position with the tonic note on top of the final chord — the most conclusive ending there is. Anything less (an inversion, or the third/fifth in the top voice) is an **imperfect** authentic cadence: still a close, but softer.

## Plagal cadence (IV–I)

The **plagal cadence** moves `IV–I` — in C major, `F–C`. It is the gentle "Amen" heard at the end of hymns, and it often *follows* an authentic cadence as a final, settling gesture. There is no leading tone driving it, so it feels warm and rounded rather than urgent.

## Half cadence (…–V)

A **half cadence** ends *on* the dominant rather than resolving to it. Any phrase that pauses on `V` — `I–V`, `ii–V`, `IV–V` — leaves the ear expecting more, exactly like a comma. It is the classic way to end the first half of a tune before the answering phrase completes the thought.

## Deceptive cadence (V–vi)

The **deceptive** (or interrupted) cadence promises the tonic and then side-steps it: `V–vi`, in C major `G–Am`. The dominant sets up every expectation of arriving home, and instead the music slips to the relative minor. It is a favourite device for extending a phrase — the "real" cadence is postponed for a few more bars.

## Putting them side by side

| Cadence | Chords | Feels like | Ends the phrase? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authentic | V – I | full stop | yes, strongly |
| Plagal | IV – I | a soft "Amen" | yes, gently |
| Half | … – V | a comma | no — leaves it open |
| Deceptive | V – vi | a surprise turn | no — postpones the close |

Notice that authentic and plagal both land on `I` (closed), while half and deceptive deliberately avoid a settled tonic (open). That open/closed contrast is what lets a composer shape long stretches of music out of short phrases.
