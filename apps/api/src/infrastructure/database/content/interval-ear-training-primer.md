## Goal

An **interval** is the distance between two pitches, and hearing intervals reliably is the foundation
of every ear skill — transcribing melodies, tuning, improvising, and reading by sound rather than by
eye. The fastest way in is to **anchor each interval to the opening of a song you already know**. Once
"that leap sounds like *Twinkle*," you can name it instantly.

## How to do it

Sing (or play) two notes, then match the leap to its reference song. Ascending references:

| Interval | Semitones | Reference song (first two notes) |
|---|---|---|
| Minor 2nd | 1 | *Jaws* theme |
| Major 2nd | 2 | "Happy Birthday" |
| Minor 3rd | 3 | "Greensleeves" / "Smoke on the Water" |
| Major 3rd | 4 | "When the Saints Go Marching In" |
| Perfect 4th | 5 | "Here Comes the Bride" |
| Tritone | 6 | *The Simpsons* ("The Simp-") |
| Perfect 5th | 7 | "Twinkle, Twinkle" / *Star Wars* theme |
| Minor 6th | 8 | "The Entertainer" (opening leap) |
| Major 6th | 9 | "My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean" |
| Minor 7th | 10 | *Star Trek* (original series) theme |
| Major 7th | 11 | "Take On Me" (chorus leap) |
| Octave | 12 | "Somewhere Over the Rainbow" ("Some-where") |

Steps:

1. **Start with three:** the octave, perfect 5th, and major 3rd. Play each, hum its song, then guess
   before checking. Do 10 a day.
2. **Add one interval at a time.** Only move on when the previous ones are automatic.
3. **Test both directions.** Descending sounds different — e.g. a descending perfect 4th opens "Amazing
   Grace" ("A-ma-"), a descending major 3rd opens "Swing Low, Sweet Chariot."
4. **Sing back** what you hear before naming it — the voice cements the sound faster than the ear alone.

## Common mistakes

- **Leaning only on songs.** Use them as training wheels, then challenge yourself to name intervals
  cold.
- **Ignoring direction.** Ascending and descending are separate skills — drill both.
- **Confusing near neighbours** (P4 vs P5, M6 vs m7). Contrast the pair directly until the difference
  is obvious.

## How to progress

Move from single intervals to short two- and three-note melodies, then whole phrases. Connect what you
hear to theory using the [Circle of Fifths reference](/circle-of-fifths-reference), and reinforce
pitch relationships by singing [Major Scales in All Keys](/major-scales-all-keys) as you play them.
