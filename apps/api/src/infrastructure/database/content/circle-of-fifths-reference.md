## What the circle of fifths is

The circle of fifths is a single diagram that arranges all twelve major (and minor) keys around a
clock face, ordered by the **perfect fifth**. Move one step **clockwise** and you go *up* a fifth,
adding one sharp; move one step **counter-clockwise** you go *up* a fourth (down a fifth), adding one
flat. It is the master map of key signatures, relationships, and chord movement.

## How to read it

Start at the top (12 o'clock) with **C major έΑΦ no sharps, no flats** έΑΦ and travel clockwise:

- **Clockwise (sharps add one at a time):**
  `C έΗΤ G(1έβψ) έΗΤ D(2έβψ) έΗΤ A(3έβψ) έΗΤ E(4έβψ) έΗΤ B(5έβψ) έΗΤ Fέβψ(6έβψ) έΗΤ Cέβψ(7έβψ)`
- **Counter-clockwise (flats add one at a time):**
  `C έΗΤ F(1έβφ) έΗΤ Bέβφ(2έβφ) έΗΤ Eέβφ(3έβφ) έΗΤ Aέβφ(4έβφ) έΗΤ Dέβφ(5έβφ) έΗΤ Gέβφ(6έβφ) έΗΤ Cέβφ(7έβφ)`
- At the bottom the keys meet as **enharmonic** pairs (same sound, different spelling): `B/Cέβφ`,
  `Fέβψ/Gέβφ`, `Cέβψ/Dέβφ`.

Two memory aids:

- **Order of sharps:** `F C G D A E B` έΑΦ "Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle."
- **Order of flats:** the reverse έΑΦ `B E A D G C F` ("BEAD" + "GCF").

Each major key also has a **relative minor** sharing its key signature, sitting just inside the
circle έΑΦ a minor third *below* the major (its 6th scale degree). So `C` major pairs with `A` minor,
`G` major with `E` minor, and so on.

## How to use it

- **Find a key signature fast.** Count the steps from C: `A` major is 3 clockwise, so 3 sharps.
- **Transpose.** Rotate a progression around the circle to shift it into a new key.
- **Build progressions.** Chords next to each other on the circle sound closely related έΑΦ the iiέΑΥVέΑΥI
  and IέΑΥIVέΑΥV progressions are all near neighbours.
- **Practise scales in order** so each new key changes by only one accidental.
