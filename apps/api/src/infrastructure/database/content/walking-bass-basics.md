## What "walking" means

A **walking bass line** is a smooth, mostly stepwise line of steady quarter notes — one note per beat — that outlines the chords while keeping the swing feel moving forward. It's the heartbeat of jazz, and the same principles serve blues and swing. We'll build one over a **ii–V–I** in C major, the most important progression in jazz: `Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7`. Standard bass tuning `E–A–D–G`.

## The one unbreakable rule: root on beat 1

Whatever else you do, **play the chord's root on beat 1** of each new chord. That single note tells the listener the harmony has changed. Everything between downbeats is your freedom to connect smoothly to the *next* root.

```
Beat:  1     2     3     4    | 1 (next chord)
Note:  Root  ...   ...   ...  | Root
        ↑ chord tone            ↑ approach the next root
```

## The four beats of a bar

A reliable recipe for each bar:

- **Beat 1** — the **root** (always).
- **Beats 2 & 3** — other **chord tones** (3rd, 5th, 7th) or scale notes that move by step.
- **Beat 4** — an **approach note** that leads by a half step or a step into the next bar's root.

That beat-4 approach is the glue. The strongest is a chromatic (half-step) approach from just above or below the target root.

## A worked ii–V–I line

Over `Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7` (one chord per bar) in C:

```
| Dm7              | G7               | Cmaj7   |
  D    A    F    A♭   G    B    D    D♭   C ...
  1    5    ♭3   ↓    1    3    5    ↓    1
  root chord tones ↑approach  root  tones ↑approach root
```

Read it beat by beat:

- **Bar 1 (Dm7):** `D – A – F – A♭`. Root on beat 1, then chord tones (`A` the 5th, `F` the ♭3rd), then `A♭` on beat 4 — a chromatic half step **down** into the next root, `G`.
- **Bar 2 (G7):** `G – B – D – D♭`. Root, then chord tones (`B` the 3rd, `D` the 5th), then `D♭` on beat 4 — a chromatic half step **down** into `C`.
- **Bar 3 (Cmaj7):** lands on the root `C` on beat 1, resolving the line.

Those beat-4 half-step approaches (`A♭→G`, `D♭→C`) are what make the line sound intentional and connected rather than random.

## Techniques for smoothness

- **Approach notes:** a half step above or below the next root is the most common and strongest connector; a scale step also works.
- **Prefer steps over leaps.** A walking line should mostly *walk*, not jump. Occasional octave leaps add variety but shouldn't dominate.
- **Use chord tones on strong beats (1 and 3),** passing/approach tones on weak beats (2 and 4).
- **Keep it steady.** Even, swung quarter notes with consistent note length and volume are more important than fancy note choices.

## Practice approach

1. First play *only roots* on beat 1 of each chord through the progression — get the changes solid.
2. Add the fifth on beat 3.
3. Fill in beat 2 with a chord tone.
4. Finally add a chromatic approach on beat 4. You've built a full walking line one layer at a time.

## Try it

Loop `Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7 – Cmaj7`, one chord per bar, at 80 BPM on **/tools/metronome** with a swing feel. Play only roots on beat 1 for four passes, then add the fifth, then a beat-4 chromatic approach into each next root. Keep the quarter notes dead steady. Check chord-tone locations on **/tools/fretboard** and review the seventh-chords lesson so you know which notes belong to each chord.
