## What a triad is

A **triad** is a three-note chord built by stacking two thirds. Its members have names:

- **Root** — the note the chord is named for and built from.
- **Third** — a third above the root; it decides *major vs. minor*.
- **Fifth** — a fifth above the root; it decides *stability*.

Stack `C`, then a third up (`E`), then another third up (`G`) and you have a **C major** triad: `C–E–G`.

## The four qualities

The two thirds inside the triad can each be major (M3, 4 semitones) or minor (m3, 3 semitones). The combination sets the quality:

```
Quality       Lower 3rd   Upper 3rd   Root-to-5th   Example
Major         M3          m3          Perfect 5th   C–E–G
Minor         m3          M3          Perfect 5th   C–E♭–G
Diminished    m3          m3          Diminished 5  C–E♭–G♭
Augmented     M3          M3          Augmented 5   C–E–G♯
```

Major and minor triads are consonant and common; diminished and augmented triads contain an unstable fifth and sound tense.

## Inversions: which note is in the bass

A triad is in **root position** when the root is the lowest sounding note. Move the root up an octave and a different chord tone sits in the bass — that is an **inversion**. The notes are the same, so it is still a C major chord, but its voicing and function shift.

```
Root position:   C–E–G   (root C in bass)
First inversion: E–G–C   (third E in bass)
Second inversion:G–C–E   (fifth G in bass)
```

## Figured bass shorthand

Classical harmony labels inversions with **figured bass** — numbers showing the intervals above the bass note. The full figures reduce to a familiar shorthand:

- **Root position** — intervals of a 5th and 3rd above the bass → figure `5/3`, usually written as nothing at all.
- **First inversion** — a 6th and a 3rd above the bass → `6/3`, shortened to **`6`**.
- **Second inversion** — a 6th and a 4th above the bass → **`6/4`**.

So a C major chord with `E` in the bass is a "six chord"; with `G` in the bass it is a "six-four."

## Roman numerals plus inversion

Combine the two systems and you can describe any diatonic chord precisely. In C major:

- `I` — C major, root position (`C–E–G`).
- `I6` — C major, first inversion (`E–G–C`).
- `V6/4` — G major, second inversion (`D–G–B`).

The lowercase/uppercase of the Roman numeral shows quality; the added figure shows the inversion. This is the notation you will use to analyse the pieces in the catalogue.

## Why inversions matter

Inversions let the bass line move smoothly instead of leaping between roots, and they change a chord's stability. A root-position tonic feels final; a `6/4` chord is unstable and typically resolves onward. Composers choose inversions largely to control **voice leading** — how each line moves from chord to chord.
