## Goal

Exercise No. 1 opens Charles-Louis Hanon's *The Virtuoso Pianist* (1873), the most famous
finger-drill collection ever written. Its single aim is **finger independence and evenness** — training
each finger, especially the weaker 4th and 5th, to strike with equal weight and timing. Master it and
scales, arpeggios, and passagework all feel steadier.

## How to do it

The pattern is a rising-and-falling figure that skips one note, so the hand sits over a **sixth**
(`C` to `A`), not a fifth. Both hands play in parallel, two octaves apart.

1. **Right-hand ascending figure:** `C E F G A G F E` with fingers `1 2 3 4 5 4 3 2`. Notice the thumb
   jumps a third (`C`→`E`); fingers `2-3-4-5` then step by seconds.
2. **Shift up one scale step and repeat:** `D F G A B A G F`, same `1 2 3 4 5 4 3 2` fingering. Keep
   climbing (`E G A B C…`) up two octaves.
3. **Left hand plays the identical shape** two octaves lower, perfectly together.
4. At the top, reverse the pattern and descend back to the start.
5. **Tempo:** begin around ♩ = 60 with a metronome. Lift each finger high and play *forte* with a
   firm, even touch. Hold the wrist quiet and level.
6. Repeat the whole exercise 2–3 times without stopping.

## Common mistakes

- **Uneven 4th/5th fingers.** They tend to sound weak or late. Slow down until every note is equal in
  volume, or the drill reinforces the imbalance.
- **Tension.** A clenched wrist or raised shoulders means you are pushing too fast or too hard. Shake
  the hand out and drop the tempo.
- **Rushing the turn.** The note where the figure changes direction (finger 5) is where sloppiness
  creeps in — give it the same clarity as the rest.

## How to progress

Ramp the metronome up a few clicks only once every note is clean — Hanon suggests eventually reaching
♩ = 108 or faster. Then continue to Exercises 2 and 3, which extend the same idea. Practise with a
steady click from [/tools/metronome](/tools/metronome), and pair this with the
[C Major Scale — Two Octaves](/c-major-scale-two-octaves) to apply your evenness to real scales.
