## Borrowing the pull of the dominant

In any major key only one chord is naturally a dominant seventh — the `V7` built on scale degree 5, which pulls strongly home to the tonic. A **secondary dominant** takes that same pulling power and aims it at a *different* chord in the key, briefly making some other degree feel like a temporary home.

We write it as **V/x**, read "five of x". A `V/V` is "the dominant of the dominant" — the chord that would be `V` if the real `V` were the tonic for a moment. Every diatonic chord except the diminished `vii°` can be tonicised this way.

## Building one

To find `V/x`, ask: *what is the dominant seventh a fifth above the target chord?* Then borrow it. In C major:

| Symbol | Chord | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| V/V | D7 | G (V) | D7 is the dominant of G |
| V/ii | A7 | Dm (ii) | A7 is the dominant of D minor |
| V/vi | E7 | Am (vi) | E7 is the dominant of A minor |
| V/IV | C7 | F (IV) | C7 is the dominant of F |

Notice `V/ii` is `A7`, not the diatonic `Am`. Raising the third of `A` to `C♯` turns the key's `vi` chord into a real dominant seventh with its own leading tone.

## The chromatic giveaway

The reason a secondary dominant sounds so strong is that it introduces a note **outside the key** — the raised third that becomes the target's own leading tone. In `A7 → Dm`, the `C♯` is that leading tone: it wants to rise a half-step to `D`, exactly the way `B` rises to `C` in a normal `V–I`. That single chromatic pitch is what your ear latches onto as extra colour.

Because the tonicisation is momentary, the key does not actually change. As soon as the secondary dominant resolves, the music is back in C major — you have simply borrowed a stronger approach to one chord.

## Hear it in a progression

The progression below is a workhorse: `C – A7 – Dm – G7 – C`. The `A7` is `V/ii`; instead of drifting into `Dm`, the harmony arrives there with a hard pull, because `A7`'s `C♯` leans up into `D`. Then the ordinary `G7–C` closes with an authentic cadence.

## Chains of dominants

Secondary dominants can string together, each one tonicising the next: `E7 → A7 → D7 → G7 → C`. Every chord is the dominant of the one that follows, so the whole passage tumbles downhill toward the tonic. This "dominant chain" powers countless jazz turnarounds and Romantic-era sequences.
