## What an interval is

An **interval** is the distance between two pitches. Naming an interval takes two pieces of information: its **size** (a number) and its **quality** (a word like *major*, *minor*, *perfect*). Get both, and you have named the distance exactly.

## Size: count the letter names

Size comes from counting letter names inclusively, starting on the lower note as "1."

- `C` up to `E` spans C‚ÄìD‚ÄìE = three letters, so it is a **third**.
- `C` up to `G` spans C‚ÄìD‚ÄìE‚ÄìF‚ÄìG = five letters, so it is a **fifth**.
- A note to itself is a **unison** (1); eight letters up is an **octave** (8).

Notice that the accidentals (sharps/flats) do **not** change the size. `C`‚Äì`E`, `C`‚Äì`E‚ô≠`, and `C`‚Äì`E‚ôØ` are all "some kind of third," because they all span three letter names.

## Quality: count the semitones

Quality tells you exactly how wide the interval is, measured in **semitones** (half steps ‚Äî the smallest distance on a keyboard). Two families of qualities exist:

- **Perfect** applies to unisons, fourths, fifths, and octaves.
- **Major / minor** applies to seconds, thirds, sixths, and sevenths.

## Altering the quality

Shrink or stretch an interval by a semitone and its quality changes by one step:

- A **major** interval made one semitone smaller becomes **minor** (major 3rd `C‚ÄìE` ‚Üí minor 3rd `C‚ÄìE‚ô≠`).
- A **minor** or **perfect** interval made one semitone smaller becomes **diminished**.
- Any interval made one semitone larger becomes **augmented** (perfect 5th `C‚ÄìG` ‚Üí augmented 5th `C‚ÄìG‚ôØ`).

The full ladder for changing a semitone at a time:

`diminished ‚Üí minor ‚Üí MAJOR ‚Üí augmented`
`diminished ‚Üí PERFECT ‚Üí augmented`

## Consonance and inversion

Perfect intervals and major/minor thirds and sixths sound **consonant** (stable, restful); seconds, sevenths, and the tritone sound **dissonant** (tense, wanting to move). When you flip the two notes so the lower becomes the upper, you **invert** the interval. Inversions follow two tidy rules: the sizes add up to 9 (a 3rd inverts to a 6th, a 2nd to a 7th), and quality flips ‚Äî major‚Üîminor, augmented‚Üîdiminished, perfect stays perfect. So `C‚ÄìE` (major 3rd) inverts to `E‚ÄìC` (minor 6th).

## Compound intervals

Intervals larger than an octave are **compound**. A 9th is an octave plus a 2nd, an 11th an octave plus a 4th, and so on ‚Äî useful vocabulary once you start reading extended chords.
